Executive Summary

The Corbett Claims website is in better shape than most small-business pre-launch sites I've seen. The single-page index.html already has a strong content structure (How It Works, Services, Team, Service Area map, FAQ, Contact), structured-data markup for local search, a working Formspree contact form, and a mobile-friendly layout. What's missing is mostly plumbing: a real domain pointed at the site, a professional email address, analytics, a sitemap, a privacy policy, and — most important for an insurance/auto-collision business — a fully optimized Google Business Profile. Total realistic first-year cost is roughly $85–$110 if you self-manage hosting and email forwarding, or $160–$185 with a full Google Workspace mailbox.

Top Recommendation

Host on Cloudflare Pages via GitHub integration, with Google Workspace for email, prioritizing Google Business Profile setup before SEO content work.

The order matters: Google Business Profile drives far more calls and assignments for a local service business than on-page SEO does. Get the profile claimed, optimized, and collecting reviews before spending time on meta tags and sitemaps.

3. Should We Use Cloudflare?

Short answer: yes. Cloudflare is the single largest free upgrade you can give your website. Here's what it actually does, in plain English:

DNS (the phonebook)

DNS turns corbettclaims.com into a server address. Cloudflare's DNS is one of the fastest in the world. Free. For Corbett Claims: people typing your domain reach the site quickly, anywhere in the country.

CDN (a copy of your site, everywhere)

Cloudflare caches your pages on 300+ servers globally. A visitor in Houston, Atlanta, or Phoenix loads the site from a nearby server, not Baton Rouge. For Corbett Claims: your service-area states (TX, MS, AL, FL, OH, IL, NV) all see a fast site.

Free SSL (the padlock)

Cloudflare automatically issues and renews the HTTPS certificate that makes browsers show the padlock. No expirations to remember. For Corbett Claims: the contact form looks trustworthy and Google won't flag the site as insecure.

DDoS protection & Bot Fight Mode

Cloudflare absorbs malicious traffic before it reaches your site, and blocks known bad bots. Free. For Corbett Claims: spam form submissions and bandwidth-draining bots are filtered out automatically.

Pages (the hosting itself)

The actual website storage and delivery. Free. For Corbett Claims: every git push to the main branch redeploys the site in about 30 seconds.

Web Analytics

Privacy-friendly, no cookies, no banner needed in most jurisdictions. Free. For Corbett Claims: see how many people visited, where they came from, what pages they viewed — without the legal headache of Google Analytics.

Recommended Cloudflare configuration

  1. Sign up for a free Cloudflare account at dash.cloudflare.com.
  2. Add the domain (corbettclaims.com or .net) to Cloudflare. It will auto-import existing DNS records.
  3. Change nameservers at the domain registrar (where you bought the domain) to the two Cloudflare nameservers Cloudflare gives you. This is the one-time step that "moves" DNS to Cloudflare. Allow up to 24 hours for propagation — usually it's done within an hour.
  4. Connect the GitHub repo via Cloudflare Pages → "Connect to Git." Build command: leave blank. Build output directory: / (the root). It auto-deploys on every push.
  5. Add the custom domain to the Pages project. Cloudflare issues SSL automatically (1–5 minutes).
  6. Enable Bot Fight Mode (free) under Security → Bots.
  7. Enable Web Analytics (free) and paste the one-line snippet into index.html before </body>.
  8. Set SSL/TLS mode to "Full (strict)" under SSL/TLS → Overview.
  9. Optional but recommended: turn on "Always Use HTTPS" and "Automatic HTTPS Rewrites" under SSL/TLS → Edge Certificates.
Gotcha

Changing nameservers is the one moment when DNS could briefly hiccup. Schedule it for an off-hours window (e.g., a Saturday morning). Keep a copy of your current DNS records before switching — especially any MX records for existing email — so nothing gets lost. Cloudflare's onboarding wizard imports most records automatically, but always double-check.

4. Domain & Email

The domain

The structured data on the current site references assignments@corbettclaims.net, which means the .net domain appears to already be owned. Decisions to make:

  • Do you also own corbettclaims.com? Most insurance carriers, attorneys, and individuals will type .com first. If you don't own it yet, buy it now (~$12/yr). Set .net to redirect to .com, or vice versa — pick whichever is "the real one" and make the other an alias.
  • If you only want one, the .com is the conventional, trusted choice for U.S. small businesses. The .net still works fine but is less common today.
  • Where to buy or transfer: Cloudflare Registrar (at-cost, ~$10.44/yr for .com, no markup, no upsells) is the best option once your domain is on Cloudflare DNS. Porkbun and Namecheap are also reputable. Avoid GoDaddy upsells.
  • If you already own the .net through another registrar, you can leave it there and just change nameservers to Cloudflare. No need to transfer the registration unless you want to.

5. SEO Essentials

For a local service business — insurance appraiser, auto-collision specialist, anyone who serves a specific geography — SEO is mostly two things: (a) your Google Business Profile, and (b) basic on-page hygiene. Don't get lost in keyword research before doing those two.

The single most important thing: Google Business Profile

According to multiple local-search studies, optimized Google Business Profiles attract up to 70% more visits than businesses without one — and for many local services, the GBP listing matters more than the website itself. Google's local ranking algorithm uses three factors: Relevance (does the business match the search?), Distance (how far from the searcher?), and Prominence (how well-known, how many reviews?).

  • Claim/create the listing at business.google.com. Free.
  • Verify by postcard (Google mails a code to your service address — takes 5–14 days).Plan ahead: don't wait until launch day.
  • Set primary category: Insurance agency or Appraiser (try both; pick whichever shows higher search volume for "auto appraiser Baton Rouge").
  • Add secondary categories: Insurance adjuster, Auto appraiser, anything else relevant.
  • Fill in: hours (Mon–Fri 9–6), phone, website URL, full address, languages (English + Spanish), service area (all 8 states from your site).
  • Add 10+ high-quality photos: exterior, interior, work-in-progress, equipment, Ronnie and Annie.
  • Write a 750-character business description with the phrases customers search: "independent auto appraiser," "Baton Rouge," "heavy equipment appraisal," "RV appraisal."
  • Turn on Messaging and Q&A so prospects can contact you directly from search results.
  • Ask your last 10 satisfied clients for a Google review. Send them the direct review link (Google generates one in the dashboard). Reviews are the single biggest local-SEO lever.

On-page SEO (prioritized)

  1. Title tags & meta descriptions: already present and well-written on the current page. As you add more pages (Services, FAQ, etc.), give each a unique title (60 chars) and description (155 chars).
  2. Schema.org LocalBusiness markup: already implemented as ProfessionalService. Consider adding AutoBodyShop or InsuranceAgency as a secondary @type if those keywords are valuable.
  3. Local citations (NAP consistency): "NAP" = Name, Address, Phone. Make sure these are identical on the website, GBP, Yelp, BBB, Yellow Pages, Foursquare, Angi, and any insurance-industry directories. Inconsistency hurts rankings.
  4. Sitemap.xml: add a simple sitemap.xml at the repo root listing every page. Submit it to Google Search Console.
  5. Robots.txt: add a minimal robots.txt allowing all crawlers and pointing to sitemap.xml.
  6. Mobile-friendly: already in good shape. Re-test with Google's Mobile-Friendly Test after launch.
  7. Page speed: the page is large-ish at ~37 KB plus Leaflet (~150 KB). Optional: loading="lazy" on the team photo (already there), defer Leaflet load until the map is in viewport.
  8. Image alt text: the team photo and map fallback already have alt text. Keep that habit for any new images.
  9. Internal linking: when you add subpages (e.g., a "Heavy Equipment Appraisal" page), link to them from the main page and from each other.

First 30 days SEO actions

  1. Day 1–3: Claim Google Business Profile, request postcard verification, fill in everything you can immediately.
  2. Day 4–7: Submit the site to Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools. Upload sitemap. Request indexing of the homepage.
  3. Day 7–14: Create listings on Yelp, BBB, Angi, Yellow Pages — with identical NAP info. List on insurance-industry directories: Find an Appraiser directories run by groups like the Independent Automotive Damage Appraisers Association if you're a member.
  4. Day 14–21: Postcard arrives → verify GBP → ask 10 past clients for Google reviews via the direct review link. One good email blast can produce 5–10 reviews if you make it easy.
  5. Day 21–30: Add the first GBP post (a 300-word update about a recent project or a seasonal reminder), upload 10+ photos, respond to any reviews that have come in.

6. Analytics, Monitoring & Legal

Analytics

Two realistic options for an informational site:

  • Cloudflare Web Analytics Recommended — Free, no cookies, no tracking pixels, doesn't require a cookie banner in most jurisdictions. Shows visits, page views, top pages, referrers, countries, devices. Less detail than Google Analytics, but more than enough for a small-business informational site. One-line script tag.
  • Google Analytics 4 — Free, much more detailed (conversion funnels, audience segments). Requires a cookie banner if you have any EU or California visitors. Slightly heavier on the page. Better if you're running paid ads and need conversion tracking.

Uptime monitoring

UptimeRobot free tier checks your site every 5 minutes from multiple regions and emails you if it goes down. Takes 90 seconds to set up. No reason not to have it.

Accessibility

The current site is in good shape: semantic HTML, ARIA labels on the menu, alt text on images, sufficient color contrast in the brand palette. Quick wins after launch:

  • Run a free WAVE accessibility scan on the live URL.
  • Test keyboard navigation: can you tab through the whole page and reach every link/button?
  • Make sure the contact form's error states (Formspree's default) are readable by screen readers.

Legal: Privacy Policy, Terms, Cookie Banner

For a Louisiana small business with a contact form that collects names, phone numbers, and email addresses, here's the realistic legal picture:

  • Privacy Policy: strongly recommended even though there's no specific Louisiana statute requiring one for a small claims-services business. Any time you collect personal information through a form, you should disclose what you do with it. Use a free generator like Termly or Iubenda. Add as privacy.html, link in the footer.
  • Terms of Service: optional for an informational site. If you start taking online payments or formal assignment requests via the site, add one.
  • Cookie banner: not legally required for U.S.-only traffic if you're not using tracking cookies. Cloudflare Web Analytics doesn't use cookies, so no banner needed. The moment you add Google Analytics, you'll want a banner.
  • CCPA (California) and GDPR (EU): almost certainly not in scope for a Baton Rouge claims business serving the South. CCPA technically only applies to businesses with $25M+ revenue or 100K+ California consumers. GDPR applies if you deliberately target EU users — which you don't. A short privacy policy covering the basics is sufficient.
  • ADA accessibility lawsuits: a real risk for any U.S. business website. Mitigation is straightforward: keep the site accessible (alt text, semantic HTML, keyboard navigation, sufficient contrast). The current site is already in good shape; the WAVE scan above is the main check.

7. Pre-Launch & Launch-Day Checklist

A concrete sequence. Each item should take 10–30 minutes. The whole thing can be done in a weekend if everything cooperates.

Pre-launch (the week before)

  • Confirm the domain. Buy corbettclaims.com if you don't own it. Decide on .com vs .net as primary.~$12/yr at Cloudflare Registrar or similar.
  • Create a Cloudflare account. Add your domain. Note the two assigned nameservers.
  • Change nameservers at your registrar to the Cloudflare ones. Wait for propagation (usually <1 hour, up to 24 hours).
  • Set up Cloudflare Pages. Connect the GitHub repo. Build command: blank. Output: /. Test that the *.pages.dev preview URL works.
  • Add the custom domain to the Pages project. Confirm SSL goes green (1–5 minutes after the DNS change is live).
  • Add a CNAME file to the repo if you ever want to switch to GitHub Pages — not needed if you're staying on Cloudflare Pages.
  • Add sitemap.xml and robots.txt to the repo. Commit and push.
  • Set up Google Workspace (or Cloudflare Email Routing as the free starter). Update DNS MX records. Verify by sending and receiving a test email.
  • Create a privacy.html page using Termly or a similar generator. Link in the footer.
  • Final content sweep: spellcheck, dates correct, phone numbers match everywhere, no Lorem ipsum.

Launch day

  • Confirm DNS is fully propagated by visiting corbettclaims.com in an incognito window. Should load the site, padlock should show.
  • Test the contact form one more time with a real submission. Confirm delivery.
  • Test on mobile — iPhone Safari, Android Chrome. Tap "Call Now," tap the email link, fill out the form.
  • Set up Google Search Console → verify ownership via DNS TXT record (Cloudflare makes this trivial) → submit sitemap → "Request indexing" on the homepage.
  • Set up Bing Webmaster Tools — import settings from Search Console with one click.
  • Install Cloudflare Web Analytics — paste the snippet into index.html before </body>.
  • Run Google Lighthouse on the live URL. Aim for Performance ≥ 85, Accessibility ≥ 90, Best Practices ≥ 90, SEO ≥ 95.
  • Verify Open Graph preview at opengraph.xyz — share preview should show the og-logo image.
  • Validate structured data at Google Rich Results Test.
  • Set up UptimeRobot with a 5-minute check on the homepage URL.
  • Submit to Google Business Profile. If verification postcard hasn't arrived yet, at least pre-fill everything.

The first week after launch

  • Soft-launch to friends & family / repeat clients. Ask them to "try to break it" — every weird browser, every screen size.
  • Email past 10 clients with a friendly note about the new site and a direct GBP review link.
  • Announce on social if you have any presence (Facebook, LinkedIn). One post with a screenshot of the site.
  • Create Yelp, BBB, Angi listings with consistent NAP info.
  • Check analytics after 48 hours — confirm tracking is firing and you're seeing real visits.

8. Recurring Maintenance

A static informational site is one of the lowest-maintenance things in business. Realistically:

Monthly (15 minutes)

  • Skim the analytics — top pages, top referrers, anything weird.
  • Glance at Google Search Console for crawl errors or new keywords.
  • Check Google Business Profile for new reviews → reply to every one (positive or negative).
  • Post one GBP update (a recent project, a seasonal reminder, a photo).

Quarterly (1 hour)

  • Run a broken-link check with a free tool like Dead Link Checker.
  • Update business hours or contact info if anything has changed.
  • Add 2–3 new photos to GBP.
  • Refresh any "as of [year]" copy in the footer.
  • Confirm the SSL certificate is auto-renewing (it should — but verify the padlock).

Yearly

  • Renew the domain registration (set to auto-renew at the registrar — but verify the credit card on file is current).
  • Bump the footer copyright year (currently shows 2026).
  • Review the privacy policy for any changes in your data practices.
  • Re-run Lighthouse and the WAVE accessibility scan.
  • Audit content: is anything stale? Have services changed? Add a fresh testimonial or two.

Dependencies to keep an eye on

The site uses two external libraries: Leaflet 1.9.4 (pinned via subresource integrity hashes — safe) and Google Fonts. Leaflet rarely needs updating for a static map like this. If you ever bump the version, update the integrity= hashes too.
